Kindling the light...

In Sunlit Grace

There was a light once—not the kind

that coaxes flowers from the earth,

nor some frail dawn, soft and resigned,

but light that knew its worth.

It cut through lies like a naked blade,

left no shadow room to plead,

scorched the meek where they prayed,

and named the rot beneath the green.

Men called it cruel. Too bright, they said.

Too sharp. Too much. They turned their faces,

let the dimness grow instead,

let the twilight mask the traces

of all they feared to see unmade.

And so it died—not with a sigh,

but like a king in ambush laid

low by those who feared the sky.

Now the dark is thick with whispers,

thick with hands that clutch and smother.

You will miss the light that blistered,

when the hush eats up another

truth. Another voice. Another name.

You will beg for fire.

But dead light does not rise again—

only ash. Only pyres.

Heed this, then, before the end:

a world that kills its brightest flame

does not fade. It does not bend.

It drowns. And it deserves the same.
